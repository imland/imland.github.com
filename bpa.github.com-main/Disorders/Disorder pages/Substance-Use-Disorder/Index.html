Addiction to substances such as alcohol or drugs.

Substance use disorder (SUD) is a treatable, chronic mental health condition characterized by the compulsive use of alcohol or drugs despite severe, harmful consequences. It alters the brain's structure and function, leading to impaired control, social problems, risky behavior, and physical dependence. SUD causes significant impairment in daily life, including health issues and inability to meet responsibilities.

Symptoms

Key Behavioral and Psychological Symptoms Impaired Control: Using more of the substance or for a longer time than intended; having an intense desire or craving; and making multiple failed attempts to cut down or stop. Time Allocation: Spending a significant amount of time obtaining, using, or recovering from the effects of the substance. Social Impairment: Failing to fulfill major obligations at work, school, or home; neglecting responsibilities (e.g., neglecting children or chores); and reducing or giving up important social, occupational, or recreational activities. Risky Use: Continuing to use the substance despite knowing it is causing persistent or recurrent physical or psychological problems, such as depression, anxiety, or high blood pressure. Dangerous Behavior: Using the substance in hazardous situations, such as driving under the influence, engaging in unsafe sex, or using dirty needles. Physical Warning Signs Tolerance: Needing to use a significantly larger amount of the substance to achieve the desired effect, or experiencing a diminished effect with continued use of the same amount. Withdrawal: Experiencing physical or psychological symptoms (e.g., nausea, shaking, sweating, anxiety) when reducing or stopping use. Appearance Changes: Bloodshot or glassy eyes, pupils larger or smaller than usual, unexpected weight loss or gain, and a lack of interest in personal grooming. Physical Effects: Impaired coordination, tremors, slurred speech, or unexplained odors on the breath, body, or clothing. Symptoms by Substance Type Alcohol: Drinking alone, frequent blackouts, or needing to drink in the morning to function. Opioids (Heroin, Painkillers): Needle marks, sleeping at unusual times, vomiting, and constipation. Stimulants (Cocaine, Meth): Hyperactivity, euphoria, irritability, anxiety, and extreme, rapid weight loss. Inhalants: Watery eyes, impaired vision, rashes around the nose/mouth, and headache. Hallucinogens (LSD, PCP): Bizarre, irrational behavior, and rapid shifts in emotions.
