Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a chronic, severe brain disorder causing psychosis—hallucinations (often hearing voices), delusions, disorganized speech, and impaired cognitive function. Affecting roughly 1 in 345 adults worldwide (0.29%-1%), it typically develops in late adolescence or early twenties, with earlier onset in men. It is managed with long-term, consistent treatment.

Symptoms

Hallucinations: Experiencing sensations that are not real, most commonly hearing voices, but also seeing, feeling, or smelling things that are not there. Delusions: Strongly held false beliefs not based on reality, such as believing one is being watched, followed, or controlled, often causing fear and paranoia. Disorganized Thinking and Speech: Difficulty organizing thoughts, leading to disjointed, incoherent, or fast, rambling speech. Disorganized or Unusual Behavior: Erratic, inappropriate, or childlike behaviors, including erratic movements or lack of movement (catatonia). Negative Symptoms: Reduced ability to function normally, including: Avolition: Lack of motivation, leading to difficulty starting or finishing tasks. Blunted/Flat Affect: Decreased emotional expression, such as monotone voice or limited facial expressions. Social Withdrawal: Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities.
