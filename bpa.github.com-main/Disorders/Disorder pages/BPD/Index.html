Borderline Personality Disorder(BPD)

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) is a mental health condition characterized by intense, unstable emotions, impulsivity, unstable self-image, and tumultuous relationships. Driven by an intense fear of abandonment, individuals often experience rapid mood swings, chronic emptiness, and self-harming behaviors. While caused by a mix of genetics and environmental factors like trauma, BPD is treatable, with many improving over time through therapy.

Symptoms

Core Symptoms of BPD Fear of Abandonment: Intense, often irrational fears of being abandoned or left alone, leading to frantic efforts to prevent it. Unstable Relationships: A pattern of intense, volatile relationships, often alternating between extreme closeness ("idolization") and dislike ("devaluation"). Unstable Self-Image: Rapidly shifting goals, values, and career aspirations, or a chronic, deep-seated sense of not knowing who you are. Impulsive Behavior: Dangerous or self-destructive behaviors, such as reckless spending, risky sex, substance abuse, or dangerous driving. Emotional Volatility: Intense, rapid mood swings that can last from a few hours to a few days, featuring extreme joy, irritability, or shame. Self-Harm or Suicidal Behavior: Recurrent suicidal threats, gestures, or behaviors, including self-mutilation or cutting. Chronic Emptiness: Persistent feelings of boredom or being "empty" inside. Intense Anger: Inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling temper. Paranoia or Dissociation: Temporary paranoid thoughts or severe dissociation (feeling detached from oneself or reality) under stress.
